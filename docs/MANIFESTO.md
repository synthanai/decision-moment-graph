# 📋 DMG Manifesto

## The Declaration

**Store decision moments, not raw chats, objects or data.**

---

## The Problem

In the AI era, context has become cheap but meaning has become expensive.

We have:
- **RAG systems** that retrieve facts but not judgment
- **Chat histories** that capture conversation but not commitment
- **Meeting notes** that record what was said but not what was decided
- **Approval workflows** that track "yes" but not "why"

The result: Organizations make the same mistakes repeatedly because decisions rot in chat threads, email chains, and forgotten Confluence pages.

**Isolated reasoning is the enemy.**

---

## The Insight

Decisions are not artifacts. They are *living structures* with:

1. **Temporal identity** — They exist in time, with before and after
2. **Provenance** — They have a history of how they came to be
3. **Dissent** — They carry the tension of unresolved disagreement
4. **Reversibility** — They have conditions under which they would change
5. **Outcomes** — They produce results that can be compared to expectations

A decision is not a point. It is a *graph of moments*.

---

## The Vision

DMG establishes a new class of context for the AI era:

> **Decision Moment Graph**: Structured records of commitments, tradeoffs, and reversibility—designed for machines to synthesize and humans to audit.

When you store decisions as structured objects:
- **LLMs can reason** over your decision history, not just your documents
- **Auditors can trace** exactly what was known, when, and by whom
- **Teams can learn** from comparing predictions to outcomes
- **Leaders can calibrate** their judgment through explicit feedback loops

---

## The Principles

### 1. Decisions Are First-Class Objects
A decision is not a conclusion to a chat. It is a structured artifact with schema, version, and provenance.

### 2. Dissent Is Data, Not Drama
Disagreement captured structurally becomes the raw material for learning. Disagreement suppressed becomes the seed of failure.

### 3. Governance Scales With Reversibility
The rigor required for a decision should be proportional to how hard it is to undo. RAMP encodes this.

### 4. Reversibility Must Be Concrete
A decision without a rollback plan is a gamble. DOORS makes reversibility explicit before commitment.

### 5. Predictions Are Testable
If you can't say what you expect to happen with what confidence, you're not deciding—you're hoping.

### 6. Every Decision Deserves An Outcome Check
Learning happens when you compare what you predicted to what actually happened. No check = no learning.

### 7. Provenance Is Non-Negotiable
Every change to a decision must be logged. MOMENT makes decisions inspectable—technically and legally.

## The Movement

DMG is not a product. It is an **open standard**.

- **MIT Licensed**: The spec, schema, and validator are free forever
- **Community Governed**: DIPs (DMG Improvement Proposals) evolve the standard
- **Interoperable**: Any tool can produce and consume DMG files

We invite you to:
- Use DMG in your tools and workflows
- Contribute to the specification
- Build on the open standard
- Join the community of decision governors

---

## The Commitment

We pledge to keep the core standard:
- **Open** — MIT licensed, forever
- **Simple** — Human-readable JSON, no proprietary formats
- **Extensible** — Custom fields allowed, core fields stable
- **Auditable** — Every event logged, hash-chained, inspectable

## The Call

You are not a stenographer. You are a **decision governor**.

Start today:
1. Create one MEMO for your next important decision
2. Add three real options (not two + "other")
3. Record one dissent (even if you have to argue with yourself)
4. Set an outcome check date
5. Compare your prediction to reality

**The future belongs to those who decide well—not just those who decide fast.**
